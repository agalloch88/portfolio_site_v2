I have interviewed hundreds of data engineers and been on the receiving end of enough loops to recognize the pattern. The highest paying roles are not looking for trivia. They are looking for evidence that you can build pipelines that stay reliable, model data that stays understandable, and communicate tradeoffs without drama. If you can do those three things consistently, $200K plus offers follow. This playbook is how I prepare people for those interviews and how I evaluate candidates when I am on the panel.

## What data engineering interviews actually test

Most candidates assume the interview is a knowledge test. It is not. It is a signal test. Interviewers are trying to answer five questions quickly:

1. Can you write production grade SQL and reason about data correctness under pressure?
2. Can you design a pipeline that survives real constraints like late data, backfills, and cost?
3. Can you model data in a way that scales across teams and evolves without chaos?
4. Can you build reliable ETL code and understand performance tradeoffs in Python or PySpark?
5. Can you lead with judgment, not just execution, when requirements are fuzzy?

You do not have to be perfect in all five. You do need to be strong in at least three and show baseline competence in the rest. Senior and staff roles tilt harder toward system design, data modeling, and behavioral judgment. Mid level roles tilt toward SQL and coding. If you walk in knowing which signals matter most for your target level, you can focus your preparation and avoid the common trap of over preparing one area and under preparing the rest.

## The five question categories you will be tested on

### 1. SQL window functions and CTEs

This is the fastest, most objective filter. A good SQL prompt separates out candidates who have been working with real data from those who only do shallow analysis. Expect window functions, CTE layering, and edge cases such as gaps, duplicates, and late arriving data. The best answers are not just correct, they are explainable and stable.

What I look for:

- Layering: Can you decompose the problem into CTEs that are readable and debuggable?
- Window semantics: Do you understand frame clauses, ordering, and partitioning, or are you guessing?
- Correctness: Do you handle ties, nulls, and time zone issues?
- Performance awareness: Do you avoid unnecessary scans or Cartesian blowups?

Practice staples:

- Sessionization with a time gap.
- Top N per group using `ROW_NUMBER`.
- Rolling 7 day metrics with a frame clause.
- SCD Type 2 style change capture.

When I prepare, I use a three step routine. First I rewrite the question in my own words. Second I outline the CTEs before writing SQL. Third I run a quick mental test with a tiny example dataset to confirm I did not miss an edge case. That pattern keeps me from wasting time in an interview and it reads as calm confidence.

### 2. System design: Kafka to Spark to Snowflake

This is where the senior roles separate. The interviewer gives you a business problem and expects a pipeline design. The default scenario in 2026 is streaming ingestion with Kafka, processing with Spark, and storage in Snowflake. You are not graded on tool trivia. You are graded on tradeoffs, especially around freshness, reliability, and cost.

A strong answer usually includes:

- Ingestion design: topic partitioning strategy, key choice, schema evolution, and retention.
- Processing: micro batch versus continuous, handling late data, checkpointing, and exactly once semantics.
- Storage: landing raw data, bronze or staging layer, transformations, and serving tables.
- Backfills and replays: how to reprocess history without corrupting downstream tables.
- Observability: metrics, data quality checks, and alerting.
- Cost controls: warehouse sizing, auto suspend, and data pruning.

I narrate a baseline architecture, then I ask questions out loud. What is the SLA? How late does data arrive? What is the cost sensitivity? What are the failure modes? If the interviewer does not answer, I state a reasonable assumption and move forward. That habit signals maturity, and it keeps the conversation focused.

### 3. Data modeling: Kimball vs Data Vault

Data modeling interviews are about how you think about change. Kimball is optimized for analytics and clarity. Data Vault is optimized for auditability and change. Most strong candidates can describe both. The best candidates can justify when each approach is appropriate.

How I frame it:

- Kimball star schema works when you have stable dimensions, a clear business process, and a need for fast queries. It is simple to teach and supports self service analytics.
- Data Vault works when source systems change often, you need full history, and you want a long lived integration layer. It trades query simplicity for auditability and flexibility.

I also connect modeling choices to downstream behavior. A Kimball model is easier for BI, but it requires more discipline when sources change. A Data Vault model makes change safer, but it pushes complexity into consumption layers. If you say those tradeoffs out loud and show a simple example of hubs, links, and satellites, most interviewers will check the box.

### 4. Python ETL with pandas or PySpark

Coding interviews are not about raw algorithm tricks. They are about building reliable transformations with messy data. You should expect to clean, join, dedupe, and aggregate in a way that is readable and scalable. In pandas, I look for vectorized operations, clear joins, and cautious use of `apply`. In PySpark, I look for avoiding UDFs, using built in functions, and understanding partitioning.

Things that stand out positively:

- Writing small, composable functions.
- Using schema validation or explicit column selection.
- Handling nulls and types up front.
- Explaining performance tradeoffs, like shuffles and broadcast joins.

Things that raise concern:

- Complex logic packed into a single line.
- Hidden state or global variables.
- Heavy use of `collect` in Spark.
- No tests or no sanity checks.

I prep for this by re implementing a simple pipeline in both pandas and PySpark. I pick a dataset like orders and events, then I build a clean dimension table and a fact table. That is exactly what interviewers want to see: can you build something that would survive in production.

### 5. Behavioral STAR, with real engineering judgment

Behavioral interviews are the quiet deciders. At $200K plus, you are expected to lead through ambiguity. STAR answers help, but the key is that your story shows ownership and reflection. The interviewer is listening for how you handle tradeoffs, setbacks, and stakeholders.

I use a simple template:

- Situation: set the context in one sentence.
- Task: define what was on the line.
- Action: explain the decisions you made and why.
- Result: show the impact with numbers.
- Reflection: what you learned and how you would do it differently.

Three story types I always bring:

- A pipeline failure and how I stabilized it.
- A stakeholder conflict and how I aligned on scope or quality.
- A design decision where I chose simplicity over cleverness.

If you want to stand out, tie your story to business outcomes. A fix that saved two hours per week is fine. A change that cut customer churn or reduced a seven figure cloud bill gets attention.

## How I approach the interview loop

I treat the loop like a system, not a set of isolated problems. My goal is to give consistent signals across rounds. I want the SQL interviewer to think I am careful about correctness. I want the system design interviewer to hear that I think about operations and cost. I want the behavioral interviewer to see that I make tradeoffs with clarity.

That means I keep my language consistent. I say things like "I am optimizing for reliability over novelty" or "I will trade a bit of latency for simpler operations." Those phrases land well because they mirror real engineering priorities. I also keep a list of five or six example projects I can reuse. Each round asks a different question, but the same example, if framed right, can reinforce your strengths.

## The six week study plan

This is the schedule I recommend if you are aiming for $200K plus roles in 2026. It assumes ten to twelve hours per week. If you have less time, stretch the plan to eight or ten weeks, but keep the same sequence.

### Week 1: SQL fundamentals and window functions

- Review joins, aggregations, and filtering with edge cases.
- Drill window functions every day, especially `ROW_NUMBER`, `LAG`, `LEAD`, and frame clauses.
- Solve at least five interview style SQL problems and write down your CTE structure before you code.

Goal: be able to explain and write window function queries without hesitation.

### Week 2: CTE design and data correctness

- Practice building multi step transformations using layered CTEs.
- Focus on null handling, deduplication logic, and time zone issues.
- Take two of your own past queries and rewrite them to be more readable.

Goal: show you can design SQL that is maintainable, not just correct.

### Week 3: System design for pipelines

- Study Kafka basics: partitions, consumer groups, delivery semantics, and schema evolution.
- Design a pipeline from Kafka to Spark to Snowflake for three use cases: clickstream, payments, and product events.
- Document assumptions, SLAs, and how you would handle late data and backfills.

Goal: explain pipeline architecture in a clear, structured way without going into tool trivia.

### Week 4: Data modeling depth

- Model a sales or subscription domain using a Kimball star schema.
- Model the same domain using Data Vault concepts: hubs, links, satellites.
- Compare tradeoffs in writing, flexibility, and query complexity.

Goal: articulate when each modeling approach wins and what you would choose for different business contexts.

### Week 5: Python and PySpark ETL

- Build a small ETL project in pandas that outputs clean dimension and fact tables.
- Rebuild it in PySpark and note where performance issues show up.
- Add simple tests or validation checks and measure runtime differences.

Goal: demonstrate practical transformation skills and performance awareness.

### Week 6: Behavioral practice and mock interviews

- Draft three STAR stories and tighten them to two minutes each.
- Do at least two mock interviews, one technical and one behavioral.
- Review feedback and refine your weak category.

Goal: show calm, structured communication under pressure.

## Final notes on landing the offer

The interview gets you the offer. The offer gets you the number. If you want $200K plus, you need to manage both. The good news is that strong interview performance gives you leverage, and leverage gives you options. I always aim to interview with two companies in parallel, keep communication transparent, and use a simple line: "I am excited about the role and I am evaluating a couple of options this week." It is direct, respectful, and it usually gets the recruiter to move fast.

If you do the six week plan and keep your answers grounded in real tradeoffs, you will sound like someone who builds reliable systems, not just someone who studied. That is the difference between a good interview and a senior level offer.
